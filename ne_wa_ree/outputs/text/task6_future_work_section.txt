
6.  Future Work

6.1  Probabilistic Grade Modeling

The spatial prioritization framework presented here identifies tailings sites
with elevated probability of monazite endowment based on multiple proxy datasets:
NURE stream sediment thorium anomalies, aeromagnetic signatures consistent with
magnetite-bearing source rocks, geologic catchment composition, and historical
production volumes. What it does not produce is a quantitative estimate of
monazite grade distribution within any individual tailings pile at the precision
required for a reprocessing investment decision.

Two sites emerge from the Section 5 analysis as the immediate auger
reconnaissance targets, for distinct but complementary reasons.

Colville Placer is the only site in the study area presenting three converging
independent lines of evidence simultaneously: a coincident aeromagnetic high
consistent with magnetite-bearing detrital input, a NURE Th anomaly classified
MIXED_UNCLEAR (reflecting the geochemical ambiguity inherent in stream sediment
data — see classification gap discussion below), and HIGH-confidence topographic
and lidar indicators of preserved placer morphology. No other site achieves this
convergence. The combined priority score is 9.13/10 (Section 5.4). The proxy-
based NdPr estimate of 203 t sits at the lower end of the site range, but the
break-even NdPr price of $81/kg — well below the current market price — means
the economic threshold is achievable at moderate grade. Auger drilling here is
not primarily a discovery program; it is a program to confirm what the
converging proxy evidence strongly implies is present, and to generate the
in-situ grade observations needed to convert the conceptual exploration target
into a NI 43-101 or JORC Inferred Resource.

Sanpoil River Placer presents the opposite risk profile. Its proxy-based NdPr
estimate of 429 t is the largest single-site figure in the dataset — approximately
2.1× the Colville Placer estimate — but it carries LOW confidence in the
prioritization framework due to the absence of a magnetic co-indicator and
reliance on stream sediment Th signal alone. No aeromagnetic high is present to
corroborate detrital magnetite input from a monazite-bearing source. This is not
disqualifying: monazite placers without a strong magnetic response are well
documented in fluvial settings where heavy mineral sorting has separated monazite
from magnetite over transport distance (Strezov et al. 2017). But it means the
grade uncertainty range is wide and cannot be narrowed further by any additional
desktop analysis. Sanpoil River Placer represents the highest-upside, highest-
uncertainty site in the study area. An auger program here is specifically designed
to collapse grade uncertainty before any larger investment decision is warranted.

Addressing this gap more broadly requires stochastic subsurface modeling applied
to both priority sites. The appropriate methodological framework integrates the
geochemical proxy data compiled here with in-situ drill hole, trench, or bulk
sample data to produce full probability distributions of monazite grade and
volume, rather than the point estimates and wide uncertainty ranges that are the
maximum achievable from stream sediment proxy data alone. Specifically, future
work should apply Bayesian geostatistical approaches (Dowd 1994; Caers 2011;
Pyrcz & Deutsch 2014) to encode physically informed priors about the spatial
distribution of heavy mineral concentration in fluvial tailings: the physics of
hydraulic sorting that governs monazite accumulation in a tailings pile during
active processing operations is well characterized (Strezov et al. 2017),
providing a physically grounded prior that would update efficiently on sparse
in-situ observations. Such an approach would produce an uncertainty-quantified
distribution of grade at depth — the basis for a formal exploration target
estimate and, ultimately, a NI 43-101 or JORC Code Inferred Resource — rather
than the conceptual exploration target presented in Section 5.3.

MONAZITE Classification Gap

A critical data limitation must be documented explicitly before any auger program
is designed. Within the NE Washington NURE subset analyzed here (1,045 samples),
cerium (Ce) is present in only 35% of records and lanthanum (La) in only 49%.
The Th-Ce-P elemental triplet required for definitive monazite mineral
classification — as distinct from thorite, uranothorite, or other Th-bearing
phases — could not be confirmed from the stream sediment data alone. The
consequence is unambiguous in the classification results: all 61 Th-anomalous
samples were assigned either MIXED_UNCLEAR (25 samples) or THORITE_UTHO (36
samples); zero samples returned a confirmed MONAZITE classification.

This result should not be misread as evidence against monazite. The absence of a
confirmed classification is not the absence of monazite; it is the absence of
the multi-element data needed to confirm it. Stream sediment Th anomalies in
metamorphic core complex catchments of this type are physically consistent with
monazite as the dominant carrier phase (the metamorphic petrology argument
developed in Section 4.3 is unaffected by this gap), but stream sediment
geochemistry alone is insufficient to resolve the mineral speciation. This gap
precisely defines what the auger program must deliver: bulk samples from the
priority sites with full REE + P + actinide assay, enabling both automated
mineralogy (MLA/QEMSCAN) and geochemical triplet classification at in-situ
grade. The NURE data establishes where Th is anomalous; the auger program
establishes what mineral is carrying it.

The difference between a proxy-based spatial prioritization and a probabilistic
in-situ grade model is the difference between knowing where to look and knowing
whether it is worth looking there before committing to an extensive sampling
program. Emerging probabilistic subsurface modeling platforms designed specifically
for critical minerals exploration settings are beginning to provide this capability
at the site scale (see e.g., Wellmann & Caumon 2018; de la Varga et al. 2019 for
the methodological underpinning of uncertainty-quantified geological models). The
computational tools for integrating sparse geochemical observations with
physics-informed structural priors now exist; the limiting factor for the sites
identified here is in-situ sampling data, not analytical capability. Future work
should proceed in two stages: (1) targeted shallow sampling (auger drilling or
trenching) at the two priority sites identified above — Colville Placer and
Sanpoil River Placer — to generate in-situ grade observations at a density
sufficient to initialize a geostatistical model and resolve the monazite
classification gap; (2) application of a probabilistic grade modeling workflow
to those observations to produce site-scale estimates with explicit uncertainty
bounds.

Cost guidance: A reconnaissance sampling program of 20–30 auger holes to 3m
depth at each priority site, with full REE + P + actinide assay on each
interval, is estimated at $68,000–$102,000 per site including mobilization
(based on published rates for similar programs in the Pacific Northwest, at
$2,000–$3,400 per hole; Western Mineral Group 2023; US Critical Minerals 2024).
The total field program for both Colville Placer and Sanpoil River Placer is
estimated at $136,000–$204,000. This sampling investment would reduce grade
uncertainty from the current ±50% to approximately ±15–25%, resolve the
monazite classification ambiguity in the NURE proxy data, and make the
exploration target estimates in Section 5.3 sufficient to support a preliminary
economic assessment.

6.2  Co-placer Heavy Mineral Characterization

The current study focuses on monazite as the target REE carrier. Future
mineralogical characterization of priority tailings sites should employ automated
mineralogy (MLA/QEMSCAN or similar) on representative bulk samples to quantify
the full heavy mineral assemblage: monazite, magnetite, ilmenite, zircon, and
any other accessory phases. The economic case for reprocessing improves
substantially if multiple heavy mineral co-products can be recovered from a single
processing pass. Magnetite and ilmenite, in particular, are recoverable by
straightforward magnetic and gravity separation with no radioactive waste
considerations, and may contribute meaningfully to project economics at the grade
levels implied by the aeromagnetic data at sites such as Colville Placer.

6.3  Hard Rock Source Verification

The detrital source characterization in Section 4.3 identified metamorphic core
complex metapelites as the dominant upstream contributor to NURE thorium anomalies
in the study area, consistent with the presence of monazite as an accessory phase
in high-grade metamorphic assemblages. Verification of this interpretation would
benefit from U-Pb geochronology and Lu-Hf isotope analysis of detrital monazite
grains separated from representative tailings samples, which would provide both
confirmation of the metamorphic source and age information useful for regional
provenance studies (Rasmussen & Muhling 2007; Holder et al. 2015). This is a
lower-priority analytical investment relative to in-situ grade sampling but would
substantially strengthen the peer-reviewed publication record for the district.
