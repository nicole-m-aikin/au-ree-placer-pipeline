
3.3  Co-placer Mineral Characterization and Economic Rationale

3.3.1  Rationale for Multi-commodity Assessment

The economic viability of reprocessing a tailings pile for REE content depends not
only on the monazite grade and tonnage, but on whether the co-recovery of additional
valuable heavy minerals makes the processing economics viable at the site's specific
grade. Monazite is rarely the sole heavy mineral of economic significance in placer
tailings: the same hydraulic sorting processes that concentrate monazite (density
5.1 g/cm³) also concentrate magnetite (5.2 g/cm³), ilmenite (4.7 g/cm³), and zircon
(4.7 g/cm³) in the same heavy mineral assemblage. Sites where monazite co-occurs
with recoverable magnetite and ilmenite have a materially better economic profile
than those containing monazite alone, because:

  (1) Magnetite and ilmenite carry no radioactive liability. Both minerals can
      be recovered and sold as non-radioactive products without NRC oversight.
      Their processing pathways — magnetic separation for magnetite, gravity and
      magnetic separation for ilmenite — are mature, low-cost, and fully
      commercialized. Co-product revenues from magnetite (iron ore) and ilmenite
      (TiO₂ feedstock) can offset the cost of thorium waste management associated
      with the monazite processing stream, reducing the effective cost of the REE
      recovery operation.

  (2) Magnetite is independently valuable. Global iron ore demand is enormous and
      stable; magnetite concentrates command premium pricing (~$120-140/tonne Fe
      concentrate) over hematite products because of their superior suitability
      for direct reduction steelmaking processes. A tailings pile containing 2–5%
      magnetite in a 500,000-tonne pile represents 10,000–25,000 tonnes of iron
      concentrate — a potentially independent revenue stream even without REE
      recovery.

  (3) Ilmenite is the primary feedstock for the TiO₂ pigment industry and for
      titanium metal production for aerospace alloys. The United States is
      substantially dependent on imports for both. Ilmenite co-recovery from placer
      tailings would contribute to domestic titanium supply chain security, a
      recognized national security priority (USGS 2024 Critical Minerals List).

3.3.2  Geophysical Detection of Co-placer Magnetite

Aeromagnetic surveys detect variations in the total magnetic intensity of the
Earth's field caused by subsurface variations in magnetite content. Magnetite is
strongly ferromagnetic; even small concentrations produce measurable anomalies
relative to the regional background field. Areas of magnetite-rich placer
concentrate produce local positive magnetic anomalies that are detectable from
airborne aeromagnetic surveys flown at standard mineral exploration line spacing
(100-200m line spacing, 60m mean terrain clearance).

For the northeastern Washington study area, the USGS aeromagnetic compilation
(Blakely et al. 1999; Griscom & Mabey 1988) provides regional coverage at
~500m effective resolution. Magnetic highs above the mean+2SD threshold in this
compilation were identified and spatially intersected with the MRDS mine site
inventory and NURE Th anomaly locations to flag sites with evidence for both
monazite and magnetite enrichment (Section 4.1 and Figure 1).

3.3.3  Spectral Detection of Ilmenite (EMIT/ASTER)

Ilmenite has a distinctive near-infrared spectral signature distinguishable from
silicate minerals and iron oxides in hyperspectral imagery. The NASA EMIT
(Earth Surface Mineral Dust Source Investigation) instrument aboard the
International Space Station provides global hyperspectral coverage at 60m spatial
resolution with sufficient spectral range (380-2500nm) to detect ilmenite
endmembers through spectral unmixing. Where EMIT coverage is unavailable, ASTER
TIR data (90m resolution) provides supplementary mineral mapping capability.

Coverage analysis for the northeastern Washington study area indicates that EMIT
data is available for portions of Okanogan and Ferry counties acquired in 2023–2024.
Full spectral unmixing analysis for ilmenite endmembers was conducted on available
scenes; results are incorporated into the ilmenite probability layer in the
integrated priority tier GeoJSON (Section 5.5, Figure 7).
