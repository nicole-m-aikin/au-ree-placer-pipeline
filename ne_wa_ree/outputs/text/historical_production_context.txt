--- SANPOIL RIVER PLACER (Sanpoil District, Ferry / Okanogan County) ---

From Bulletin 677 index: "Placers of the Sanpoil district, panning tests of [page] 140"
(PDF index line ~9044). "West Fork of Sanpoil River, placer claims on [page] 103" (line 9127).

SANPOIL DISTRICT CONTEXT (pages 103–140):
- The Sanpoil district is described as "almost unglaciated" (p.107, PDF lines 5056–5068),
  which is unusual for the Colville Reservation. A "comparatively feeble branch lobe
  pushed down the Sanpoil Valley at least as far as Iron Creek" — below Bridge Creek,
  ice was only a few hundred feet thick and occupied only the narrow valley bottom.
- The lack of glacial overriding means: (a) pre-glacial placer concentrations may be
  partially preserved, and (b) there is less reworking by glaciofluvial processes compared
  to the western districts.

WEST FORK SANPOIL RIVER / GOLD CREEK PLACERS (Park City District, page 103):
- Crounse mine on Strawberry Creek: "an output of $100 in placer gold is reported"
  (PDF line 4113–4115). This is a minimal historical figure from pre-1912 working.
- Gold Creek: placer claims noted at page 103 of the original bulletin. Gold Creek drains
  northeastward to West Fork of Sanpoil River. The basin is described as ~100 square
  miles of "mountainous area" (p.86–87).
- Typical Park City district geology: Covada group argillite and quartzite (Carboniferous),
  Colville granite (Cretaceous), overlain by andesitic lavas (Tertiary). Glacial drift
  "at least 75 per cent of the basin of Gold Creek is overspread by a mantle of glacial
  drift that is mainly thin and patchy but in places forms extensive deposits 100 feet
  or more thick" (p.90, PDF line 4213–4215).

PARK CITY DISTRICT PRODUCTION (page 88, PDF lines 4108–4118):
- Mountain Boy mine: "four or five carloads that averaged $60 to the ton" (lode mine,
  not placer). 4 carloads × 20 tons × $60/ton = ~$4,800.
- Crounse mine placer (Strawberry Creek): $100 total placer gold production.
- In 1912, minimal activity — annual assessment work only on ~20 claims.

SANPOIL RIVER DRAINAGE GEOLOGY / SEDIMENT ROUTING:
- Sanpoil River formerly flowed NORTHWARD (reversed drainage documented, p.45).
  "A former northward flow beyond Devil's Elbow, about 18 miles north of Keller, is
  fairly well shown." (PDF lines 2051–2053). Devil's Elbow is a diorite gorge.
- After drainage reversal, the Sanpoil enlarged rapidly by headward erosion, capturing
  north-flowing streams. This reversal history means placer gold distribution may not
  follow modern drainage gradients simply.
- Sanpoil River presently flows SOUTH to Columbia River at Keller.

SANPOIL LODE SOURCE FOR PLACERS:
- The Keller locality (Sanpoil district) has numerous quartz veins: galena, zinc blende,
  chalcopyrite in Colville granite. Gold is present "in nearly all the lodes, but in
  only a few is it sufficiently abundant to be worth taking into account" (p.108).
- Best documented gold values: Iron Creek Mining & Milling Co., 2 carloads @ "100 oz
  Ag and $2 or more in gold to the ton" (PDF line 5149–5150). This is very low Au.
- The dominant metals in Sanpoil district are Ag, Pb, Zn — placer Au potential from
  this source is LOW compared to the MCC terranes to the west and northwest.

--- GENERAL PLACER PRODUCTION, COLVILLE RESERVATION (pages 53–54) ---

From the production summary section (page 54, PDF lines 2500–2600 range):

Gold: Production figure referenced at index page 54; the text notes gold was a

COVADA DISTRICT PLACERS (near Columbia River, p.177–178, PDF lines 8359–8406):
- Gravel bars along Columbia River between mouths of Wilmont and Barnaby creeks
  (Covada district, northeast Stevens County, near Inchelium).
- Gold described as "flour gold" — "finely divided or 'flour' gold."
- Panning tests: "average of 100 particles of finely divided gold per pan, estimated
  collectively to be worth between one-eighth and one-fourth cent" per pan.
- Blue Bar Island (near mouth of Stray Dog Creek): flour gold "renewed by annual
  freshets." Estimated yield: "a miner using a sluice or rocker, with blanket riffles,
  might wash out 50 cents' worth of gold a day."
- W. Johnson placer mine (NE¼ sec. 8, T.32N., R.37E., below mouth of Stranger Creek):
    → Production: ~$100 from ~2,000 cubic yards
    → Equivalent yield: ~5 cents per cubic yard
    → A. J. Collier (USGS Bull. 315, 1907) reports: "pay streak tested by panning at
       several places, estimated to contain from 40 cents per cubic yard at the richest
       spot to 5½ cents at the poorest."
    → Pay streak: 1–3 feet of medium to fine textured gravel, beneath 4–8 feet of sand.
- Nespelem district placers: "possibility of" noted in index (p.72); no production data.

--- LOOMIS QUADRANGLE PLACER DEPOSITS ---

PRIMARY PLACER SECTION (PDF lines 8015–8024, Bulletin 64 p.121):

"PLACER DEPOSITS

The gold-bearing gravels along the lower course of the Similkameen River were among the
first deposits in the area to attract the attention of prospectors. The volume of auriferous
gravel was not large, and the deposits were essentially worked out in the latter part of the
nineteenth century, although desultory sluicing and 'sniping' continues to the present day.
The presence of platinum was reported by Huntting (1956); the source of the platinum is
possibly iron-bearing pyroxenite."

INTERPRETATION:
- Location: Lower Similkameen River (flows east into Okanogan River near Oroville, WA).
  The Loomis Quadrangle covers T.38–40N., R.25–26E. (extreme northern Okanogan County,
  Canadian border area).
- Grade/Tonnage: NOT given quantitatively. "Volume of auriferous gravel was not large"
  and "essentially worked out" by ~1900.
- Platinum: Reported by Huntting (1956 — WA Div. Mines and Geology Bull. 37). Source
  inferred as iron-bearing pyroxenite of the Similkameen composite pluton (alkalic/
  ultramafic body). This is a significant REE-pathway indicator: pyroxenite/malignite
  of the Similkameen is an alkalic intrusive with known ultramafic components.

RICH BAR PLACER (PDF lines 7095–7139, Bulletin 64 Table 7):
- Location: SW¼NW¼ sec.11, T.40N., R.26E.
- Type: Old placer at site of Rich Bar Lode (worked "as far back as 1859")
- Metals: Au
- Note: "Ref. infers that Rich Bar Lode is at site of old Rich Bar Placer, worked as far
  back as 1859." Production figures NOT given in Bulletin 64.
- The associated Rich Bar Lode: Cu, Zn, Ag, Pb; 150-ft shaft with drifts.

LOOMIS QUADRANGLE GEOLOGY RELEVANT TO PLACER SOURCES:
- Three major plutons: Loomis pluton (calc-alkalic), Toats Coulee pluton (intermediate),
  Similkameen composite pluton (alkalic, with pyroxenite/malignite/syenite gneiss).
- The Similkameen composite pluton is the most relevant placer source — it contains:
    * Pyroxenite with disseminated magnetite (Fe₂O₃ = 7.3–10.2%)
    * Malignite and shonkinite (alkali-rich, potentially REE-bearing)
    * Greisen, aplite, alaskite phases
    * Shankers Bend diatreme: fenite + carbonatite dikes noted (PDF lines 4686–4691)
      "Less abundant are carbonate masses... believed to be carbonatite dikes because of
       their many physical similarities to the feldspathic dikes and their association
       with the alkalic rocks of the diatreme."
- The Shankers Bend diatreme DOES contain carbonatite dikes within the Loomis Quadrangle
  (NE corner of quadrangle, near Oroville). This is the nearest confirmed carbonatite
  to the NE WA study area (~50 km NW of the Okanogan MCC).

VOLUMETRIC / GRADE DATA FOR LOOMIS PLACERS:
- No cubic yard volumes or oz/yd³ grades are provided in Bulletin 64 for the
  Similkameen placer or Rich Bar placer.
- The text states only that the volume "was not large" and deposits were "worked out."
- Platinum presence suggests PGE + Au assemblage from ultramafic source (pyroxenite).

NOTES ON DATA QUALITY
============================================================

1. Both PDFs are early-20th-century surveys (1918, 1972) with OCR. Line-by-line reads
   were conducted to locate placer data. The systematic index search of Bulletin 677
   confirms "Hunters" is definitively absent.

2. Bulletin 677 placer production data is sparse — most districts record only panning
   tests, not volumetric production. The Colville Reservation was primarily a lode
   mining area; placers were considered marginal.

3. For the Sanpoil River placer specifically, the full page 140 section was referenced
   in the index but its content ("panning tests") suggests it is a qualitative description
   without production tonnage figures. The West Fork Sanpoil placer claims (p.103) are
   proximal to the Park City lode district.

4. The most relevant placer data for this pipeline (Hunters, Sanpoil main stem) requires
   supplemental sources beyond these two bulletins.
